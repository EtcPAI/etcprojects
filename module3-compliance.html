The seven KYR domains

Your compliance footprint covers seven domains. Privacy, cybersecurity, device management, physical security, AML, licensing and conduct, and the Four Pillars of ethics. Each one is a place AI can introduce a problem when it does not know where you operate.

The Four Pillars
Transparency, Consent, Confidentiality, Stewardship. The ethical floor under everything else.
Why this is the first step. The remaining four units capture the specifics. Which regulators apply to you, where you practice, what you sell, what designations you hold. AI uses all of it to keep its output inside the right lane. Skip the context and AI produces plausible content that does not match your reality.
A note for the whole team: the compliance context file belongs to everyone. The licensed advisor anchors it, and every team member relies on it the moment they load AI to draft anything that touches a client. Licensed Assistants drafting letters. Admins building summaries. Associates preparing meeting talking points. The file is shared infrastructure.

A Licensed Assistant catches AI naming the wrong regulator

Priya is a Licensed Assistant in an Alberta-based practice. She drafts a privacy disclosure paragraph for a new client onboarding letter and asks AI to clean up the wording. The cleaned-up version references "the Securities and Exchange Commission" as the body overseeing client information practices.

Priya stops at the line. The SEC is a US regulator. It has no authority over a Canadian insurance practice. AI defaulted to a familiar regulator name because the prompt did not tell it otherwise. She loads the compliance context file (which has OPC, CAI for Quebec, and OIPC Alberta selected), regenerates the paragraph, and the language now references PIPEDA and the Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner of Alberta. The file did the work the prompt did not.

Before and after: what jurisdiction context actually changes

The prompt Draft a short privacy disclosure email to a new client explaining how we handle their personal information.
Without a compliance file
Dear [Client], Thank you for choosing our firm. We take the privacy of your personal information seriously. Your information is collected in accordance with applicable privacy legislation in Canada and is used to provide financial advisory services to you. We retain your information for as long as necessary to provide our services and meet our legal obligations. You may contact us at any time to access or correct your personal information. If you have any questions, please contact our privacy officer. Sincerely, [Advisor]
With Quebec / Law 25 loaded
Hi [Client first name], A quick note on how we handle your personal information. Because we serve clients in Quebec, our privacy practices follow Law 25 (the Act respecting the protection of personal information in the private sector). A few specifics that matter for you: - We collect only the information we need to provide you with advisory services - We let you know in advance when your information will leave the province (cross-border data transfers) - You can access, correct, or ask us to delete your information at any time - Our designated privacy officer is [name], and you can reach them at [contact] If a privacy concern ever comes up, you have the right to file a complaint with the Commission d'accès à l'information du Québec (CAI). Talk soon, [Advisor first name]
What changed: generic Canadian framing became Quebec-specific. Law 25 named. CAI named. The deletion right and cross-border notice obligations that Law 25 requires beyond PIPEDA show up where the original missed them. The compliance file did that.
For practices that serve clients across multiple provinces: tick every province where you are licensed to operate. Quebec gets handled separately because Law 25 has stronger consent and transparency requirements than PIPEDA, and AI needs to know to apply them.

An Admin catches a fabricated credential

Marcus is the Admin for a two-advisor practice. He uses AI to draft introductory paragraphs for a new client onboarding package, including short bios for each advisor. AI's draft for one of the advisors includes "Certified Financial Strategist (CFS)." Marcus does not recognize the designation. He checks the advisor's actual credentials. The advisor holds CFP and CHS. There is no CFS designation in Canadian financial services. AI fabricated a plausible-sounding credential.

Marcus removes the fabricated line, replaces it with the advisor's actual designations, and adds the designation list to the practice's compliance context file so the same fabrication does not happen again. Bios that get sent to clients now match what the advisors actually hold.

A note for everyone on the team: designation accuracy belongs to every seat that produces content with someone's name on it. A fabricated credential in a draft is a misrepresentation, regardless of which seat caught it. Loading the practice's actual designations into AI prevents the mistake at the source.
