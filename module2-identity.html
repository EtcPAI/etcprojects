Why AI gets your voice wrong by default

AI is trained to write fluently using patterns from a huge body of text. Without specific input from you, it averages to the median voice across all that text. The result reads competent, generic, and faintly corporate. Knowing why this happens makes the rest of the module easier.

Why AI gets your voice wrong
≈ 1.5 min

The default voice

Without identity input, AI produces what amounts to the median voice across its training data. Polite. Hedging. Slightly formal. Words that no one would object to and no one would remember. For client-facing work in advisory practice, the median is almost always wrong.

What gets lost

  • Your specific phrases. The way you open a difficult conversation. The way you close a follow-up. The phrases your long-term clients recognize as yours.
  • Your sentence rhythm. Some advisors write in short, direct lines. Others use longer, structured paragraphs. AI defaults to whichever is most common in its training, not yours.
  • Your point of view. First-person plural (we) signals partnership. Second-person (you) signals advisory authority. Default AI drifts toward formal third-person.
  • The things you leave out. Most practice voices are defined as much by what they leave out as by what they include. AI fills those gaps with patterns from somewhere else until you tell it not to.

Before and after: what a voice file actually changes

The prompt Draft an email to a client letting them know I need to postpone next week's review meeting because of a scheduling conflict.
Without a voice file
Dear [Client], I am writing to inform you that, regrettably, due to an unforeseen scheduling conflict, I will need to postpone our previously scheduled review meeting that was set to take place next week. I sincerely apologize for any inconvenience this may cause. Please find below a list of available alternative dates and times. I would greatly appreciate it if you could review these options at your earliest convenience and let me know which works best for your schedule. I look forward to our rescheduled meeting and to continuing our productive working relationship. Warm regards, [Advisor Name]
With your voice loaded
Hi [Client first name], Quick note. Something came up on my end for next week, so I need to push our review. Sorry for the late notice. Here are a few times that work this side of month-end. Take a look and let me know what fits. If none of these work, tell me and we'll sort it out. [Times] [Advisor first name]
What changed: formal padding became conversational and direct. Three sentences where one would do became one sentence that does the work. Action-oriented close instead of a generic sign-off. Sounds like a real person who has met this client before. The voice file did that.
A short voice file outperforms a long one. Two real samples and a handful of characteristics will outperform a 500-word style guide nine times out of ten.
Why this fix is permanent. Once your voice file exists, you load it as system context at the start of every AI session. AI reads it before it writes anything. The default voice never gets a chance to take over.
A note for Licensed Assistants and Admin: the voice file matters most when work passes through more than one seat. A draft you produce using the advisor's voice file lands closer to the advisor's voice on the first pass, which means less rewrite and faster turnaround on client-facing work.

A Licensed Assistant working across two advisors

Devon supports two advisors with very different voices. Advisor A writes warm and conversational. Advisor B writes structured and brief. Each has their own voice file loaded into the practice's shared library. When Devon drafts a client follow-up on behalf of Advisor A, she loads A's voice file before prompting. When she drafts on behalf of Advisor B, she loads B's.

Before the voice files existed, Devon had to code-switch between the two advisors' styles from memory, and each advisor would end up rewriting her drafts to sound right. With the files in place, the drafts land closer to each advisor's voice on the first pass. The rewrite step shrinks. The advisors get back time. Devon's drafts get reviewed and sent rather than reviewed and rewritten.

What you stand for

Voice captures how you sound. Values capture what shapes that sound. Both belong in the file because AI uses your values to make judgment calls when a prompt does not give a clear answer.

What you stand for
≈ 1 min

Three to five lines per box is the target. Specific over abstract. "I never recommend a product I would not own myself" beats "I act with integrity."

An Associate Advisor sees values shape AI's framing

Maya is an Associate Advisor preparing talking points for a meeting where a client wants to discuss an early RRSP withdrawal to fund a renovation. Without values loaded, AI produces a balanced draft: here are the tax consequences, here are the alternatives, here is a neutral summary.

Maya's lead advisor's commitments include "I always lead with the long-term cost of a short-term decision, even when the client has already made up their mind." With that commitment loaded, AI restructures the draft. The talking points open with what the withdrawal would cost the client at retirement, present the renovation options that protect the registered savings, and respect the client's right to choose either path. Same information. Different order. Different emphasis. The client's choice is theirs to make, and the advisor's commitment shows up in how the choice gets framed.

The values did not censor AI. They shaped how AI organized the work.

Why both fields exist. Values describe what you believe about good work. Commitments describe what clients can count on from you. AI handling client-facing work needs both. Values shape its judgment. Commitments shape its behavior.
A note for everyone on the team: the lead advisor's values and commitments anchor the practice. When you draft anything on behalf of the practice, those values travel with you. Loading them into AI is how you carry the practice's standards into the work even when the lead is not in the room.
