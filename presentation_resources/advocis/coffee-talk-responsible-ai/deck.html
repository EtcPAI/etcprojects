§ i · Ethical Lens05 / 20
Before the tool, the promise

Commitments already made.

Promises don't change because a new tool showed up. They precede it, and they outlast it.

i. Consent A real choice, freely given. Not a checkbox at the bottom of a form.
ii. Confidentiality What a client tells you stays where they expect it to stay.
iii. Transparency If they saw exactly how the tool handles their information, would they be comfortable?
iv. Judgment The professional in the room is still you. The tool does not get to inherit the relationship.
Rod BuryloThe four standing promises
§ i · Ethical Lens06 / 20
Two words. Often confused.

Ethics vs. Compliance.

Compliance
What you have to do.
A consent checkbox at the bottom of a form. A policy you signed. The minimum that keeps the file closed.
Example. A line in the engagement letter that mentions "third-party tools may be used in the course of service."
Ethics
What you should do.
Explaining to your client exactly what an AI tool will do with their words, and respecting their answer when they say no.
Example. "Before we begin, I use a tool that transcribes our conversation so I can stay present with you. Here's what it captures and where it lives. Are you comfortable with that?"
The gap between themis where trust is built, or lost.
§ i · Ethical Lens07 / 20
Rod's framework · ask before adoption

Three questions before any tool.

i.
Does this honor the commitments I've already made to my client?
ii.
Can I explain how this tool handles their information, clearly, in my own words?
iii.
Would my client feel comfortable if they saw exactly how their data was being used?
If any answer is nothe tool isn't ready for your practice.

§ ii · Operational Lens10 / 20
The order matters

People. Process. Then technology.

01 · First
People.
Who is doing what.
Roles, responsibilities, accountability. Every person on the team who will touch the tool, not just the advisor whose name is on the door.
02 · Second
Process.
How the work flows.
Workflows, protocols, the documented sequence from intake to file. Where the tool fits in, and the guardrails when it does.
03 · Third
Technology.
What you actually buy.
The tool itself. Easy to procure, easy to install. The last decision, never the first.
Every technology failure has followed the same sequence: the tool arrived before the roles were defined.
Kelly GustafsonThe order is the lesson
§ ii · Operational Lens11 / 20
A vocabulary for evaluation

The AI Taxonomy.

Four categories. Different capabilities, different limitations, different risk. Treating them all the same is where practices get into trouble.

i. General Purpose ChatGPT · Copilot · Gemini · Claude Broad, public-facing models. Powerful and unbounded, which is exactly the problem with regulated information.
Risk profile
ii. Bolt-On AI inside the tools you already use Features added to your CRM, email, or document suite. The risk profile depends on what the vendor does with the data.
Risk profile
iii. Function-Specific Note-takers · transcription · summarization Built for one job and good at it. Narrower surface area, narrower risk, when configured and consented properly.
Risk profile
iv. FS-Specific Canadian compliance-aware advisor platforms Built for advisors with the rails baked in. Canadian residency, retention, audit trail. The category to grow into.
Risk profile
Risk is shaped by categoryNot by enthusiasm
§ ii · Operational Lens12 / 20
Governance before deployment

Three things on the table before the tool goes live.

i.
A documented policy.
What is permitted, what is not, what gets reviewed, and by whom. Written down, so it can be trained, referenced, and updated.
ii.
A consent framework.
A repeatable conversation, not a one-off. Plain language, recorded answer, opt-out path. Built into the client experience.
iii.
A training plan.
For every team member who touches the tool. The advisor's understanding does not transfer by osmosis to the assistant.
It sounds like a lot of work.It's less work than cleaning up after.
§ ii · Operational Lens · scenario13 / 20
Implementation without governance

Three months. Two hundred transcripts.

The setup
A practice adopts an AI note-taker. The lead advisor signs up Monday, uses it Tuesday, mentions it to the team on Friday.

No consent script. No review protocol. No discussion of what should never be recorded.
Twelve weeks later.
200 transcripts containing health information, family disputes, and third-party details.
That is what implementation without governance looks like. It rarely begins as a crisis. It begins as a free trial.

§ iii · Practical Lens17 / 20
A script you can borrow

The consent conversation.

A version Joe uses
Before we start, I want to mention something. I use a tool that records and transcribes our meeting so I can stay present with you and follow up faster. It's stored securely on our side, only I and my team see it, and you can ask me to turn it off at any moment. Are you comfortable if we use it today?
Name the tool.
Not "some AI." Tell them what it does in plain words. Record. Transcribe. Summarize.
Offer a real choice.
Mean it. If they say no, the tool stays off, and the relationship is better for it.
Record the answer.
A line in the CRM. A signed acknowledgment for first use. Repeat at material changes.

The obligation, the script structure, and what the conversation actually sounds like in a real meeting.

Request guidance in writing, store the response, and build your own governance while you wait. Your obligations exist regardless of what arrives from above.

You have an opportunity to get it right from here. Audit what's in use, by whom, for what, and build from there.

Train together, share boundaries, and make adoption a team project. A safe space to experiment matters as much as the policy itself.
