§ Section 01 · Rod's frameworki

The Three Questions, as a one-pager.

Before any AI or technology tool enters your practice, sit with these three questions. If any answer is no, the tool isn't ready. Not for you, not for your team, not for your client.

i. Does this honor the commitments I've already made to my client? Yes. Proceed to ii.
ii. Can I explain how this tool handles their information, clearly, in my own words? Yes. Proceed to iii.
iii. Would my client feel comfortable if they saw exactly how their data was being used? Yes. The tool is ready to evaluate.
If yes to all three
Move to the operational lens. The tool has passed the ethical gate. Now build the policy, the consent, the training.
If no to any one
The tool isn't ready for your practice yet. Identify what would have to change in the tool, in your process, or in your understanding, to get to yes.
§ Section 02 · Kelly's frameworkii

The AI Taxonomy worksheet.

Place every AI tool your practice uses, or is considering, into one of four categories. The category sets the risk profile, the governance requirement, and the conversation you need to have.

i. General Purpose ChatGPT · Copilot · Gemini · Claude Broad, public-facing models. The most capable, the least specialized. Risk is high specifically because the surface area is unbounded. Anything can be typed in, anything can come out.
Risk
ii. Bolt-On AI inside CRMs, email, document suites Features added to tools you already use. The data handling depends entirely on what the vendor does with it. Same vendor, different feature, different rules.
Risk
iii. Function-Specific Note-takers · transcription · summary tools Built for one job. Narrower surface area, narrower risk, and a clearer consent conversation, when configured properly.
Risk
iv. FS-Specific Canadian compliance-aware advisor platforms Built for advisors with the rails baked in. Canadian residency, retention, audit trail, regulatory awareness. The category to grow into where possible.
Risk

§ Section 03 · Joseph's practiceiii

A consent script you can borrow.

The version in the Practice Performance Lab training, with two alternates for different contexts. Read them aloud first. Adjust the words until they sound like you. This is your starting point. Make it yours.

A · The standard opening For a recurring client at the start of a review meeting.

I use an AI tool that records and transcribes our meeting so I can stay present with you and follow up faster. It's stored securely on our side, only I and my team see it, and you can ask me to turn it off at any moment. Are you comfortable if we use it today?

B · The new-client version For a first conversation, before any AI tool is engaged.

A quick note before we begin. As part of how I work, I use a few technology tools, including AI that transcribes our conversations. Here's what it does, here's what we do with it, and here's how it stays inside our practice. Before we use it, I'll walk you through a short consent form so you can see exactly what you're agreeing to and sign it only if you're comfortable. Anything you'd like me to clarify first?

C · The sensitive moment When the conversation turns to something a client would not want recorded.

I'm going to pause the recording for the next few minutes. What we're about to discuss should stay between us, in this room. You and I will both remember it. I'll start it again when we move on. Is that alright with you?

Name the tool.
Not "some AI." Tell the client what it does in plain words. Record. Transcribe. Summarize. Specifics build trust faster than vagueness ever does.
Offer a real choice.
Mean it. If they say no, the tool stays off, and the relationship is better for it. A no, once respected, is worth a thousand checkboxes.
Record the answer.
A line in the CRM, a signed acknowledgment for first use, a note when something material changes. Consent is not a moment. It's a maintained state.
§ Section 04 · Pre-deploymentiv

The pre-deployment governance checklist.

The three things that need to be on the table before any AI or technology tool goes live in your practice. Policy. Consent. Training. The order does not change.

i. A documented policy.
What's allowed, what isn't, what gets reviewed.
  • Scope: which tools, which use cases, which client information.
  • Prohibitions: data categories that may never enter the tool.
  • Review cadence: who reviews outputs, how often, in what format.
  • Incident protocol: what happens when something goes wrong.
  • Version, owner, last review date, written on the policy itself.
ii. A consent framework.
A repeatable conversation, not a one-off form.
  • A standard script for first use, in plain language.
  • A re-consent trigger when material changes occur.
  • A storage location for recorded consent in the CRM.
  • A no-friction opt-out, and a documented way to honour it.
  • A line in the engagement letter that reflects the practice, not the other way around.
iii. A training plan.
Every team member who touches the tool.
  • An onboarding session covering policy, consent, and prohibitions.
  • A practice round. Supervised use on non-sensitive material.
  • A quarterly refresh as the tool, the team, or the rules evolve.
  • A named owner inside the practice for AI & tech governance.
  • A short escalation path when an edge case appears.
