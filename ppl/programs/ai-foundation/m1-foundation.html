How AI actually works

A reset before everything else. Most of what goes wrong with AI in advisory work traces back to a misread of how AI generates its output. Getting this clear first changes how every later step lands.

How AI actually works
≈ 1 min

What AI does well

AI is a pattern-matching tool trained on a large body of text. It generates responses that sound right based on patterns it has seen before. Used well, that makes it useful for:

  • First drafts, summaries, rephrasing, and comparisons
  • Structuring information you already have
  • Thinking out loud, stress-testing ideas, and getting unstuck when a blank page is the obstacle
  • Producing a starting point quickly so you can spend your time on the parts that need judgment

Where you stay in the driver's seat

A few situations show up often enough to plan for them on day one.

  • Anything regulatory. AI can sound compliant on a Canadian topic while citing the wrong regulator, the wrong threshold, or rules from another country. Tone of confidence carries no signal about accuracy.
  • Anything client-specific. AI only knows what you tell it. When you do not bring the context, it fills the gap with patterns from somewhere else. The result reads plausible and is not yours.
  • Anything factual you have not verified. AI is willing to produce a citation, a name, a statistic, or a procedure that sounds correct and has no source.
  • Any decision that touches a client. AI can move you faster on drafts. Suitability, disclosure, conflict resolution, and the parts of advisory work that require professional judgment stay with you.
A useful frame for AI in advisory work: a sharp, fast assistant who needs supervision. Sometimes brilliant. Always supervised.

A Licensed Assistant catches a fabrication

Marcia is a Licensed Assistant. She uses ChatGPT to draft a short letter to a retired client explaining a recent change to RRIF withdrawal rules. The draft references "Section 146.3(2) of the Income Tax Act" as the section that governs the change. Marcia checks the section against the Department of Finance Canada website before sending the letter for advisor review. The section number is wrong. AI cited a section that exists but does not cover what the letter says it covers.

Marcia removes the citation, replaces it with plain language ("recent changes to RRIF rules"), and flags the source check for her advisor's review. The fabrication never reached the client. The habit that caught it — verify every specific citation against a primary source — is what every team member is building in this module.

Why this matters in Canadian financial services. AI tools can produce content that looks compliant and is not. Invented FINTRAC procedures. The wrong regulator named for a province. Fabricated AMF references. Language that violates your firm's disclosure standard. The supervisor on the output is you, every time. Build the habit on day one.
A note for Licensed Assistants and Admin: the rules in this module apply to your seat in the same way they apply to the advisor's. Always route AI-generated client-facing content through the licensed advisor before it leaves the practice.
Step 2 of 5

The Four Pillars

Four ideas govern responsible use of AI in financial services. They show up as specific behaviors across your practice every day. Every ground rule, prompt, and team standard you build later traces back to these four.

The Four Pillars
≈ 1.5 min

Transparency

Clients know what tools you use, how their information is handled, and what happens to it. No surprises.

What this looks like in practice: a disclosure conversation at onboarding, and again any time a new tool is introduced. If a meeting is being recorded or transcribed by AI, the client is told before the meeting starts.

Consent

Consent is an informed decision the client makes after they understand what is happening. A signature documents the decision. The conversation creates it.

What this looks like in practice: a real option offered before AI tools are used in the client's meeting. The client knows they can decline or withdraw consent at any time. The decision is documented in the file. Withdrawal is respected and noted.

Confidentiality

Information shared in the advisory relationship stays protected. AI introduces new pathways for that information to travel. Understand them before you use them.

What this looks like in practice: every AI tool used on client data is evaluated for data residency, access controls, and deletion capability before use. A written list of approved tools is maintained. Information is never pasted into a tool that has not been evaluated.

Stewardship

You take responsibility for every tool that touches client information. Stewardship means active oversight, with a documented process behind it.

What this looks like in practice: a tool evaluation checklist used before adoption. Periodic review of tools in use. A process for retiring tools and deleting data when a tool is no longer used.

Where each pillar fails most often. Transparency fails when clients learn about your AI use after the fact. Consent fails when it is collected as a checkbox rather than as a decision. Confidentiality fails when client data goes into a tool no one evaluated. Stewardship fails when a tool is adopted because a peer recommended it and never reviewed again.
The Four Pillars are the floor under every other choice you will make about AI. Every ground rule, every prompt, every team standard checks back against these four.
Step 3 of 5

What you do with AI, and why

Unit 1 covered how AI works. Unit 2 covered the Four Pillars that govern its use. This is where principle becomes practice. Five behaviors that turn the pillars into how you work day to day.

What you do with AI, and why
≈ 1.5 min

1. Think before you act.

Why this matters. Every AI use touches a client decision or a piece of client work. The pause before pressing send is where careful use is built. Most AI failures come from skipping this moment.

In practice. Ask whether the task is appropriate for AI before you start. Read the output before sending. When the answer involves a regulator, a number, or a client decision, verify before you trust.

2. Protect what matters.

Why this matters. Confidentiality is the bedrock of advisory work. AI introduces new pathways for client information to travel. You have to understand them before you use them.

In practice. Never paste client identifiers (full name, SIN, account numbers) into a public AI tool. Use only tools you have evaluated and approved. Keep a written record of which tools touched which information.

3. Stay current.

Why this matters. Regulations change. Tools change. Defaults change. What was good practice last quarter is not always good practice this quarter. The information AI was trained on has a shelf life.

In practice. Follow regulator updates from primary sources, not summaries. Watch for changes to your AI tools' capabilities and guidance. The Steward's Log from Etc. Projects delivers monthly updates from this program once you complete the modules. Apply them when they arrive.

4. Speak up.

Why this matters. Mistakes get worse when they are hidden. Teams get smarter when what was tried gets shared. Silence is the cost of unlearned lessons.

In practice. Report AI errors and near-misses to your team. Flag a tool that is not behaving the way you expected. Share what is working so others can build on it.

5. Choose value over speed.

Why this matters. AI is fast. Fast is useful. Fast without judgment becomes a liability. AI works best as an accelerator for work you already know how to do. It does not replace the work that needs you in the seat.

In practice. Use AI for drafts, summaries, and structuring information you already have. Reserve your time for client-facing judgment, suitability decisions, conflict resolution, and conversations only you can have.

An Admin notices what AI invented

Jordan runs operations for a three-advisor practice. Each quarter she pulls together a list of unresolved follow-ups from the previous quarter's client meeting notes. She tested an AI summarizer to see if it could speed up the work. The summary it produced was fast and clean. One line stood out: "Client expressed concerns about Quebec residency for tax purposes." Jordan did not remember any conversation along those lines, and the client lives in Ottawa. She checked the source notes. The line was not in any of them. AI invented it, plausibly, from a pattern it had seen in other contexts.

Jordan removed the line, switched back to her manual process for that quarter, and reported the near-miss at the next team stand-up. The team agreed AI summaries get a second pass against source notes before anything in them gets acted on. Speaking up works as the practice's earliest warning system. The team learns faster when near-misses get shared.

What this unlocks. Client meetings that hold attention better because AI handles routine documentation. Follow-up that lands more consistently because AI tracks commitments. Compliance that holds up better because AI monitors deadlines. Service that lands more personal because the foundation file you are building today travels with you across every tool.

What makes a ground rule worth keeping. A good rule is short, concrete, and written in your voice. It names the behavior. The principle stays implied. Your name on the file
< 1 min
Why this section matters. When you load my-ai-foundation.md into ChatGPT or Claude as system context, the AI reads who you are first: name, role, jurisdiction, lines of business. That context shapes everything it generates next. Get it right and the AI writes for your seat. Skip it and the AI writes for no one in particular.

Before and after: what the file actually changes

The prompt Draft a short follow-up email to a client about their RRIF withdrawal questions.
Without a foundation file
Dear Client, Thank you for our recent conversation regarding your RRIF withdrawal options. As we discussed, the IRS allows for several withdrawal strategies that can optimize your tax position, including the systematic withdrawal plan and lump-sum distribution methods. Please let me know if you'd like to schedule a follow-up to review these options in more detail. Best regards, [Advisor Name]
With your foundation loaded
Hi [Client first name], Following up on our conversation about your RRIF. The minimum withdrawal for the year is based on your age and the RRIF value at the start of the year. I'll send you the specific number, along with a few scenarios if you want to consider taking more than the minimum (there are tax considerations either way). Quick note before you reply: please don't include account numbers or your SIN in email. If you want to share specifics, give me a call or use the secure portal. Talk soon, [Advisor Name]
What changed: the Canadian context is right. The disclosure habit (no account numbers in email) is built in. The voice is closer to your own. The foundation file did that, and it did it without making the email sound like a compliance reminder. This is what the file you are about to generate gives you, every session, every tool.
