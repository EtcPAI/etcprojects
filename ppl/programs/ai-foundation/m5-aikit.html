Step 1 of 5

Why team standards matter

Foundation, identity, and compliance files are personal. They keep one user aligned. The Practice AI Kit is shared. It keeps the team aligned with each other.

Why team standards matter
≈ 1.5 min

Three things only a team-level Kit can solve

  • Consistency across seats. When every team member makes their own AI decisions, clients see different versions of the practice depending on whose seat their work passed through. The Kit makes consistency easier than inconsistency.
  • Escalation paths. When AI output touches something sensitive (a complex case, a regulatory edge, a high-net-worth client), every team member should know what gets reviewed and by whom. Without a written rule, escalation depends on who happens to feel uncertain that day.
  • Shared do-not-AI list. Some tasks AI should not touch. The list belongs to the practice, not to one person's discretion. New team members inherit it on day one rather than learning it after a near-miss.

Before and after: what the Kit prevents across three seats

Imagine a three-person practice. Lead Advisor, Associate Advisor, Licensed Assistant. All three use AI. There is no Kit yet.

Without a Kit

The Lead Advisor uses AI to draft client emails and treats account numbers as okay to paste because his AI tool is "private."

The Associate uses AI for suitability comparisons and sends drafts directly to clients because no one told her she had to route them through anyone first.

The Licensed Assistant uses AI to draft a response to a client complaint letter, because the Lead Advisor never said not to.

Three seats. Three different standards. Three different exposure levels. One regulator inquiry away from a problem the practice did not see coming.

With a Kit

Same practice. Same three seats. Same prompts on the same day.

The Lead's foundation file (and the Kit) names "I will never paste a client's full name, SIN, account numbers, or other identifiers into a public AI tool." Account numbers stay out of AI.

The Associate sees "AI-generated product recommendations or suitability statements before they reach a client" on the escalation list. She routes her draft to the Lead for review. The Lead reviews, the draft gets refined, the meeting prep is solid.

The Licensed Assistant sees "AI is never used to draft a regulator response, complaint reply, or legal letter without firm review" on the do-not-AI list. She stops, routes to the Lead, who handles the response with firm support. AI stays out of it entirely.

Same tools. Same prompts. Different exposure. The Kit did that.

Who builds it. A Lead Advisor or Admin drafts the Kit. The team reviews and ratifies it. Once ratified, the Kit becomes the meta-context for every AI session: loaded alongside every team member's personal files, referenced when a tool produces output the user is uncertain about.
For Lead Advisors: drafting the Kit is your work. Take the time. Pull the team in for ratification.

For Admin: often the Kit lives with you operationally. You are the one who keeps it current.

For everyone else: ratification deserves a real read. When something in the Kit does not fit, surface it now. Better to surface it before the rule has to be applied in real time.

An Associate Advisor uses the escalation rule

Sarah is an Associate Advisor at a four-person practice. She drafts talking points for a client meeting using AI. The client wants to discuss shifting from a balanced fund to a higher-equity allocation. AI's draft includes specific allocation suggestions ("a 70/30 split between equities and fixed income would align with the client's stated time horizon").

The Kit's escalation list includes "AI-generated product recommendations or suitability statements before they reach a client." Sarah recognizes the trigger and routes the draft to the Lead Advisor before the meeting. The Lead reviews, removes the specific allocation suggestion (because suitability requires the full KYC picture that the prompt did not include), reshapes the talking points around what the client wants to explore, and the meeting prep gets sharper. The trigger did its work. The suitability decision stays with the Lead, where it belongs.

An Admin stops the list from getting tested

Naveen is the Admin for a three-advisor practice. The practice receives an inquiry letter from FSRA asking for information about a recent client complaint. Naveen's instinct is to use AI to produce a first draft of the response so the Lead Advisor can edit.

Before he prompts, he checks the Kit. Item three on the do-not-AI list reads "AI is never used to draft a regulator response, complaint reply, or legal letter without firm review." Naveen stops. He flags the inquiry to the Lead Advisor immediately, who handles the response personally with the firm's compliance support team. AI plays no role in producing the response.

The do-not list is the boundary the practice agreed to in advance, before the moment of decision. Naveen did not have to weigh whether AI was "appropriate enough" in the moment. The Kit already decided.
