What makes a working prompt

A working prompt has five parts. Skip any one and you get generic output. The patterns later in this module follow this anatomy. Your custom prompts should too.

What makes a working prompt
≈ 1.5 min
Role
Who AI is being. "You are an associate advisor preparing notes for a senior advisor's review." Setting the role narrows the output and changes the register.
Context
What AI needs to know. The client situation, the meeting that happened, the product involved, the jurisdiction. As much as is true and not sensitive.
Task
The specific thing AI is producing. Not "help me write a follow-up." "Draft a three-paragraph email confirming next steps after today's needs analysis."
Format
How the output should be shaped. Length. Structure. Whether it includes headings, bullets, or runs as prose. Where it will end up.
Constraints
What AI must honor. Your foundation, identity, and compliance files. Things to avoid (jargon, specific phrasings, overpromising).

Before and after: what the anatomy actually changes

A bare prompt
Help me write a follow-up email to my client.
The same intent, written with the five parts
Role: You are drafting a client follow-up email on behalf of an Ontario CFP serving pre-retirement clients. Context: Following our annual review with Maria (first name only) on Tuesday, the topics we covered were the updated retirement income projection and the change to her CPP start date. The next steps we agreed on are: I send the updated income summary by Friday, Maria reviews and replies with any questions by the following Tuesday, and we book the next review for early September. Task: Draft a three-paragraph follow-up email that confirms what we discussed and what comes next, in a tone that matches my-identity.md. Format: Plain email. Greeting, body, closing. No bullet lists. Constraints: Honor my-ai-foundation.md, my-identity.md, and my-compliance-context.md. Do not invent commitments I did not make. Keep client identifiers limited to first name only. Flag anything that needs my review before sending.
What changed: the output now reads as a specific message to Maria, in your voice, referencing the actual meeting, with the actual next steps. The anatomy did the work.
Why this anatomy works. Each part covers something AI cannot infer reliably without you telling it. Skip Role and AI guesses. Skip Context and AI fills the gap with patterns from training data. Skip Format and you get a generic shape. Skip Constraints and your foundation and identity work never gets applied. The five parts together produce work that fits your seat.

A Licensed Assistant builds the prompt the whole team adopts

Anna is a Licensed Assistant supporting two advisors. She drafts post-meeting follow-ups for both of them, and the common case (confirm what was discussed, list the action items with deadlines, set a check-in date) shows up almost every week. She builds a working prompt that handles this case well, using the five-part anatomy and including a section that asks AI to flag any action items with regulatory implications (KYC updates, disclosure obligations) for advisor review.

She saves the prompt to the practice library with a clear "When to use" line. Three weeks later, the other Licensed Assistant in the practice picks up the prompt for her own advisor's follow-ups. Cycle time across the team drops. The prompt now carries Anna's name and the date she added it. When the practice updates its tool the following quarter, Anna is the one who tests the prompt against the new model and refreshes the constraints.

The library is a place team members build expertise out loud. Anna's contribution shows up every time someone else uses her prompt.

Step 4 of 4

Sharing across your team

How the library grows. What gets shared, what gets attributed, and how the team learns from each other's prompts.

Sharing across your team
≈ 1 min

Visibility

Every prompt in the library is visible to every team member who has registered against your license. Role tags help each seat find the prompts most relevant to their work, and everyone can see and use every prompt regardless of tag.

Attribution

Every prompt carries the contributor's name and the date it was added. When a prompt gets edited, the edit history is preserved so the team can see how the prompt has evolved. Attribution builds trust in the library because the team knows who tested what.

Tagging by tool origin

Every prompt also carries a tag for the tool it was tested against (Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini). Same prompt, different tool, often different output. Tagging the origin keeps the team from assuming portability.

A Lead Advisor catches a stale prompt

Theo is the Lead Advisor at a four-person practice. Three weeks after the team updated its AI tool to a new model version, he notices that the output from the practice's "Document drafting" prompt has gotten softer and less specific. The drafts no longer include the [VERIFY: question] flags the prompt asked for. Constraints that the prompt sets explicitly are being skipped.

Theo flags the issue at the team stand-up. He tests the same prompt against the previous model version and confirms the output had been sharper. The team rewrites the constraints section, tightens the format spec, and updates the library entry with a new tool-origin tag and a note in the edit history. The prompt name and purpose stay the same. The body got refreshed for the tool it now runs against.

Library health works as the team's early warning system. Every team member who uses a prompt becomes a quality check on whether it still does what it says it does.

The library is a living thing. Prompts get retired when they stop working. Prompts get rewritten when a tool updates. The team keeps the library current by using it, reporting what's not working, and adding what is.
For Lead Advisors: a healthy library is a sign of healthy AI use across your team. When only one or two people are contributing, the rest of the team is either not using AI or using it without sharing what's working. Both are worth a conversation.

For Associates, Licensed Assistants, and Admin: contribution is part of the work. The prompt that helped you today probably helps someone else next week.
