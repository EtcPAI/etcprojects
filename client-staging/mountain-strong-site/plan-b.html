Revision 6.0 · Western Canada & Ontario

Plan B.
The details of your life, organized in one place.

The idea behind your Plan B is to create awareness, and to ensure that even though we need to live our life Plan A, it is only prudent to have a Plan B.

We do not always see life's challenges, even if they are right in front of us or all around us; however, that does not mean they are not there simply because we cannot see them. With a strong Plan B in place, you can forget about life's challenges and enjoy your life. Your progress saves locally on your own device as you go.

Section 16of 20·Estate & wishes

The seventeen-point check.

A quick yes/no across the pieces most people get wrong. Answer honestly. Any "no" is a conversation, not a problem.

01. I have a signed will prepared by a professional.
02. I have a signed power of attorney for my financial affairs.
03. I have a signed power of attorney for my personal care.
04. I have recently reviewed my will and powers of attorney.
05. My family knows where to find my will and powers of attorney.
06. My family knows where to find my financial records, tax returns, and insurance policies.
07. My family knows where to find my digital records, online banking, subscriptions, and passwords.
08. I have planned my funeral and provided the details to my family.
09. I have a current net worth statement listing my assets and liabilities.
10. I have enough cash or life insurance to pay funeral, income taxes, legal, executor, and probate fees.
11. I have enough cash or life insurance to replace my income and maintain my family's lifestyle.
12. I have taken steps to protect assets brought into my current spousal relationship.
13. I have named beneficiaries for RRSPs, RRIFs, LIFs, TFSAs, annuities, life insurance, and pension plans.
14. I have considered making a planned gift to charity.
15. I have a document outlining my wishes for organ donation.
16. I have a succession plan in place for my business.
17. I have a buy/sell agreement with my business partner(s).

Section 19of 20·Team & handover

Executor duties.

For the person who will one day read this alone. Sixty steps, clustered into eight phases. Not every step applies to every estate; this is a guide, not a script.

If you are the executor reading this and feel overwhelmed, you are not alone. You have the right to step aside and let the named alternate take over, or to hire a corporate executor. Both are legitimate, reasonable choices.
When death occurs
01Notify the appropriate authorities: physician for an expected death, 911 for unexpected, police if suspicious, coroner outside Canada the nearest embassy.
02Arrange organ, tissue, or whole-body donation if requested.
03Arrange repatriation in the event of foreign death.
04Check on any pets or livestock that may require care.
05Secure property and make sure no lack of occupation is visible to potential thieves.
06Locate the will and review for funeral instructions.
07Assist with funeral arrangements if required.
Before initiating administration
08Assess the complexity and time commitment before proceeding as executor. The alternate may be more capable. Consider hiring a corporate executor.
09Assess your personal risk and consider executor insurance.
Initial administrative steps
10Forward mail.
11Obtain multiple original proof-of-death certificates; most organizations require originals.
12Ensure the family's immediate financial needs are met.
13Arrange insurance for any unoccupied property.
14Review marriage contracts, family law issues, dependent relief.
15Probate the will if required.
16Pay probate taxes as required.
Communicating with beneficiaries
17Communicate directly with beneficiaries; gather information and set expectations.
18Provide regular updates on the administration.
19Provide a copy of the estate summary document to entitled beneficiaries.
20Communicate with charities and residual beneficiaries.
Asset protection
21Review insurance and verify coverage on all assets.
22Keep utilities and property taxes paid on time.
23Notify banks and institutions of the death.
24Cancel all credit cards; destroy or return them.
25Notify Equifax and TransUnion of the death.
26Open an estate account; transfer all other balances into it.
Estate valuation
27Contact mortgage issuers to identify insured mortgages and cease payments.
28Locate certificates, stocks, bonds, deeds, and online accounts.
29Value and record estate assets as of the date of death.
30Determine all debts owed and all debts receivable.
31Apply for CPP / QPP death benefit.
32Contact employers regarding outstanding wages, benefits, pensions.
33Apply for personally owned, group, and affinity-group insurance benefits.
Administering the estate
34Review investment suitability; determine what to sell; consider tax, fees, and timing.
35Invest surplus cash in permissible investments until finalization.
36Assist in establishing any trusts stipulated in the will.
37Cancel CPP / OAS / GIS within 30 days. Return any additional payments.
38Apply for CPP / QPP survivor's and children's benefits.
39Advise CRA to discontinue GST / HST credits and child benefits.
40Arrange to transfer employment, health, pension, and retiree benefits.
41Return SIN card, passport, driver's license, health card; deposit refunds into the estate account.
42Defend, settle, or initiate legal actions on behalf of or against the estate.
43Advertise for creditors.
44Pay all debts and settle legitimate claims before distribution; get written receipts.
Taxation
45Obtain a copy of the most recent tax return filed.
46Seriously consider hiring a CPA. An estate may require up to six different returns.
47File all outstanding tax returns and pay required income taxes.
48Obtain tax clearance certificates from CRA (and Revenue Quebec, if applicable).
Distribution & closing
49Initiate sale of assets and transfer of property titles.
50Begin distributing assets with strict adherence to the will.
51Distribute specific bequests; get receipts from each beneficiary.
52Prepare an account of all expenses, including executor compensation.
53Arrange final distribution of all remaining assets; get receipts.
54Prepare a final accounting: assets, liabilities, income, expenses, distribution.
55Have adult beneficiaries approve the accounting and sign a release.
56Advise the bank in writing to close the estate account.
57Advise beneficiaries who receive significant new assets to get professional financial advice.
